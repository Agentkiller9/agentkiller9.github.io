Domain Admin on a live production AD

Authorised internal penetration test · Live production environment · 2025

Active Directory Red Team BloodHound Impacket Internal Pentest

# TL;DR

During an authorised internal penetration test against a live production Active Directory environment, I escalated from a low-privileged starting position to Domain Admin by chaining AD enumeration, credential-based attacks and ACL/delegation abuse. The findings were reported to the customer's system administrators and used to harden the environment.

# Context

  • Engagement type: Authorised internal AD penetration test against a live production environment.
  • Scope: A production Active Directory deployment; in-scope hosts, networks and accounts agreed in writing before testing.
  • Goal: Identify realistic attack paths from a typical low-privileged user foothold up to high-privilege compromise.
  • Reporting line: Direct to the customer's system administrator; findings were sanitised and remediations tracked.

Specific hostnames, account names, exploited misconfigurations and exact TTPs are redacted per engagement policy.

# Approach

1. Reconnaissance & enumeration

From the initial foothold, mapped the domain structure — users, groups, computers, OUs and trust relationships — using standard AD recon tooling. Cross-referenced findings against typical misconfiguration patterns documented in the MITRE ATT&CK framework.

2. Credential discovery

Identified credential-exposure opportunities through targeted enumeration of shares, scripts and service configurations. Built a working set of low-privileged accounts to pivot from.

3. Attack-path analysis

Used BloodHound to ingest collected AD data and visualise privilege-escalation paths. Prioritised the shortest realistic chain to a high-value target, factoring in detection likelihood.

4. Privilege escalation

Exploited a chain of ACL and delegation-related misconfigurations to elevate the working account's effective rights. Each step was validated on a non-production target before being executed in scope.

5. Domain compromise

Obtained Domain Admin equivalent control. Performed the minimum actions required to evidence the compromise — no further lateral movement, no data exfiltration, no persistence dropped.

# Outcome

  • A full attack-path report was delivered to the customer's system administrator, including reproducible proof-of-concept steps and screenshots.
  • Findings fed directly into the customer's hardening priorities — including a password-policy revamp driven off the report's recommendations.
  • Discoveries were used as anonymised teaching material for the customer's incoming security team.

# Lessons learned

  • Misconfiguration beats exploits. The most impactful steps in the chain weren't 0-days — they were small, accumulated ACL and delegation mistakes that BloodHound makes obvious.
  • Reporting matters as much as access. A path to DA that the defender can't reproduce is worthless. I now lead with reproducibility from step one.
  • Minimise impact. Once compromise is proven, stop. Internal engagements don't need to flex; they need to be safe and signed off.
